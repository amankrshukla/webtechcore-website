// THE METRIC THAT'S LYING TO YOU

The ROAS trap — why growing revenue can mean shrinking profit

Most e-commerce brands track one number: ROAS — Return on Ad Spend. It feels like the right metric. It’s actually the most dangerous one.

A brand running ads at 4x ROAS looks healthy on the dashboard. But if gross margin is 40%, shipping eats 15%, returns 8%, and gateway fees 2% — the real contribution margin after ad spend is negative. Revenue grows. Profit shrinks. The business scales toward failure.

MER — Marketing Efficiency Ratio

MER = Total Revenue ÷ Total Marketing Spend across ALL channels — not just the platform showing your ROAS. Top Indian D2C brands now target a blended CAC 30–40% lower than their paid CAC by mixing organic content, email, WhatsApp, and SEO alongside paid ads.

LTV:CAC — the north star of profitability

The benchmark for a sustainable D2C business is 3:1 — for every ₹1 spent to acquire a customer, you generate ₹3 in gross profit over their lifetime. Many Indian D2C brands run at 1.5–2.5x. They’re growing — but not building a sustainable business.

What we track instead of just ROAS:

✓Blended CAC by channel — Meta Ads, Google Ads, influencer, organic, email, WhatsApp — all tracked separately.
✓LTV at Month 1, 3, 6, and 12 per acquisition cohort.
✓Contribution margin per order — revenue minus COGS, shipping, returns, payment fees, and ad spend.
✓Payback period — months to recover CAC from purchases; target under 6 months.
✓New vs. repeat customer revenue split — healthy D2C brands target 40–60% repeat revenue.
// THE COMMISSION PROBLEM

The Amazon & Flipkart commission problem — and the ONDC opportunity

Amazon India and Flipkart charge 15–40% commission on every sale depending on category. A brand doing ₹10 lakh/month through Amazon pays ₹1.5–4 lakh in commissions — to a platform that owns the customer data, not you.

Every customer who buys through Amazon is Amazon’s customer. You have no email, no WhatsApp number, no purchase history. If Amazon changes its algorithm, raises fees, or suppresses your listing, your entire business is at risk.

The three-channel strategy we build:

🏪

Your own D2C website

Your highest-margin channel. Your full customer data. Your brand experience. The foundation of every sustainable e-commerce business.

📦

Marketplace presence (Amazon + Flipkart)

For discovery and reach — not your primary revenue channel. Capture new customers here, then migrate them to a direct relationship you own.

🔓

ONDC storefronts

India’s open network for digital commerce — lower-commission discovery and reach beyond the walled gardens, with the customer relationship staying yours.

We use marketplaces for what they’re good at — discovery — then build the systems (email, WhatsApp, SMS, retargeting) that move those buyers onto your owned channel where the margin and the data are yours.

Why is ROAS not enough to measure e-commerce success?+

ROAS only measures revenue against one platform’s ad spend — it ignores COGS, shipping, returns, payment fees, and your other marketing channels. A 4x ROAS can still be unprofitable. We track contribution margin per order, blended CAC, and LTV:CAC so you scale on profit, not vanity revenue.

How do you lower customer acquisition cost (CAC)?+

By reducing dependence on paid auctions. We build organic SEO, email, and WhatsApp channels that acquire and retain at near-zero marginal cost, improve creative and targeting on paid, and build retention so each customer is worth more — which lets you profitably pay more to acquire. The result is a lower blended CAC across all channels.

How do I reduce my dependence on Amazon and Flipkart?+

Build a three-channel strategy: your own D2C website (highest margin, full data), marketplaces for discovery only, and ONDC for open-network reach. We build your owned store and a migration system — inserts, email/WhatsApp capture, and retargeting — that turns marketplace buyers into direct, repeat customers you control.

Which e-commerce platform do you recommend — Shopify or WooCommerce?+

Both are excellent; the right choice depends on your catalog size, budget, and need for customization. Shopify is faster to launch and lower-maintenance; WooCommerce offers more control and lower platform fees at scale. We recommend and build on whichever fits your unit economics — then optimize it for conversion and speed.
